# Naming the Little Worlds world

## Naming philosophy inferred from the 43 OpenAI Showcase projects

The strongest Showcase names do four things:

1. **They name the experience, not the machinery.** *Glass Towers*, *Paper Glider*, *Field Day*, and *MiniTown* tell you what you will touch or enter. They do not explain the model, framework, pipeline, or API.
2. **They pair a concrete image with a simple world frame.** Recurring frames include *Lab*, *Studio*, *Town*, *Towers*, *Day*, *Arena*, and *Cartography*. Each makes the project feel like a place with rules rather than a feature bundle.
3. **They are short enough to become a wordmark.** Most memorable names are one to three words, easy to say, easy to picture, and free of qualifiers.
4. **They leave room for discovery.** *Kiln* does not say “3D ceramics storefront.” *Asterism* does not explain its card mechanics. The name creates curiosity; the tagline explains the first action.
5. **They favor nouns over claims.** They rarely promise intelligence, automation, productivity, or scale in the title. The experience proves those qualities.
6. **They encode tone through metaphor.** *Material Lab* feels exploratory. *Field Day* feels cheerful. *Scent Cartography* feels premium and poetic. The naming frame quietly sets expectations before the description begins.

For this project, the right name should suggest **a world of related things**. Its central promise is not object generation by itself. It is that every new object can be different while remaining visibly native to one learned, handcrafted language.

# Strongest five

## 1. Little Worlds

**Why it wins:** It is already the name of the project’s most defensible invention. It is tactile, visual, warm, easy to say, and broad enough to become a world. Like *Field Day*, it does not explain itself completely; the experience gives the words meaning.

### Title

**Little Worlds**

### Tagline

**A playful world where every new object speaks the same handcrafted language.**

### Short description

Little Worlds is a living design world built with Codex and GPT-5.6 Sol. Begin with a photo or a few words, create a one-of-a-kind object, and watch it arrive with the same soft forms, visible joins, and bright material language as everything around it.

### Long description

Little Worlds is an interactive 3D world built around a handcrafted visual language. The system first learns how that world is assembled—its rounded forms, captured panels, pegs, collars, colors, and seams—then uses those rules to create an unlimited family of distinct objects. A chair, lamp, plant, cabinet, or unfamiliar keepsake can take on a new silhouette without losing the visual ancestry that makes it belong.

### Demo script

- **Open:** Glide across a small Little Worlds studio filled with clearly related but non-identical objects. “Everything here comes from the same visual language.”
- **Create:** Upload a photo of an object unlike anything already in the scene. “Give it a photo, or describe something new.”
- **Reveal:** Show the evidence, proposal, shaping, and validation stages, then reveal the result. “The system does not choose a stock model. It builds a new member of the world.”
- **Place:** Pick up the object, rotate it, and settle it beside an existing piece. “Different shape. Same world.”
- **Expand:** Flash through several sibling objects or a wider scene. “Little Worlds can grow from one room into a whole world.”

### Initial prompt

Build **“Little Worlds,”** an interactive 3D world where an AI learns one handcrafted visual language, then creates an unlimited number of unique objects that all obey it. Begin with a small open studio filled with related furniture, lights, plants, storage, and keepsakes. Every piece should use the same construction grammar: softened masses, captured panels, visible functional pegs and collars, restrained seams, pale wood, ivory surfaces, and bright blue, coral, mustard, and green accents.

Let a person upload a photo or describe an object in a few words. Read the input as evidence, propose a new object, compile it from the world’s allowed forms and joins, validate it, and place it directly into the scene. Never silently replace an uncertain result with a generic stock object. The result may be an interpretation, but it must be visibly unique and unmistakably native to Little Worlds.

Make the central proof immediate: create one object, pick it up, turn it, and settle it beside existing pieces. Preserve its identity so it can be reused later, and let duplication create a sibling variation rather than a literal clone. Keep the room calm, tactile, and cinematic, with late-afternoon light and small physical responses that make every piece feel held by the world.

Prioritize visual kinship over technical explanation. A visitor should understand the invention by seeing two very different objects and immediately believing they belong together.

### Showcase page copy

**Little Worlds**  
*A playful world where every new object speaks the same handcrafted language.*

Little Worlds is an interactive 3D design world built with Codex and GPT-5.6 Sol. Starting from a photo or a few words, it creates distinct objects from a shared vocabulary of soft forms, visible joins, tactile materials, and bright color—so every new piece feels different, but nothing feels out of place.

## 2. Formfolk

**Why it wins:** It makes the objects feel like members of a family rather than outputs from a tool. It is cozy, scalable, and unusually good at expressing variation plus kinship in one word.

### Title

**Formfolk**

### Tagline

**A growing family of objects shaped by one visual language.**

### Short description

Formfolk is a playful 3D world built with Codex and GPT-5.6 Sol. Create an object from a photo or description, then watch it join a growing family of forms that share the same materials, construction, and character.

### Long description

Formfolk is a generative object world where every piece is different and every piece is related. A handcrafted Little Worlds language defines how forms soften, panels sit, joints show, colors meet, and objects hold themselves. From that shared ancestry, the system can create new furniture, lights, plants, vessels, and unfamiliar household forms without collapsing them into a repeated template.

### Demo script

- **Open:** Show several distinct objects together. “Meet the Formfolk.”
- **Create:** Describe “a tall reading lamp with a loop handle” or use a photo. “Every new form starts with a clue.”
- **Reveal:** Let the new lamp arrive with a surprising silhouette but familiar joins and colors. “It is new, but you can see where it comes from.”
- **Compare:** Place it between two existing pieces and briefly duplicate a sibling variation. “No clones. Just relatives.”
- **End:** Pull back to the studio. “A family of forms that can keep growing.”

### Initial prompt

Build **“Formfolk,”** a warm interactive 3D world populated by unique objects that share one visual ancestry. Teach the system a handcrafted Little Worlds language made from rounded silhouettes, captured panels, visible pegs and collars, clean seams, pale wood, ivory surfaces, and a small set of bright colors.

Allow people to introduce a new object with a photo or a short description. Use that clue to create a distinct member of the Formfolk rather than selecting or recoloring a stock asset. Preserve the object’s category and identity, but let its silhouette, proportions, construction details, and accents be genuinely new. When duplicated, create a sibling variation that is recognizably related without being identical.

Present everything in a calm miniature studio where objects can be picked up, rotated, placed, and collected. Keep the proof visual: a visitor should be able to compare a chair, lamp, and plant and recognize their shared ancestry before reading how the system works.

### Showcase page copy

**Formfolk**  
*A growing family of objects shaped by one visual language.*

Formfolk is an interactive 3D world built with Codex and GPT-5.6 Sol. It turns photos and descriptions into original objects, then shapes each one through the same Little Worlds vocabulary so new silhouettes arrive as relatives, not replicas.

## 3. Commonworld

**Why it wins:** It communicates the largest ambition most directly. The name says that an entire world can share a common design language without becoming visually repetitive.

### Title

**Commonworld**

### Tagline

**Build an endless world from one shared language of form.**

### Short description

Commonworld is a generative 3D world built with Codex and GPT-5.6 Sol. It creates endlessly varied objects from one handcrafted system of shapes, joins, materials, and color, giving every new piece a natural place in the world.

### Long description

Commonworld begins with a simple idea: a world feels coherent when everything inside it shares an underlying language. Its Little Worlds grammar defines how objects are shaped, assembled, colored, and held together. The system can then create an unlimited range of unique objects—from small keepsakes to furniture and architecture—while preserving the visual rules that make the world recognizable.

### Demo script

- **Open:** Start wide enough to show the shared language across furniture and architecture. “A world is more than a collection of objects.”
- **Focus:** Move close to the pegs, collars, panels, and seams. “It has rules you can feel.”
- **Create:** Generate a new object from a surprising photo. “Commonworld uses those rules to make something it has never seen here before.”
- **Place:** Set it into the scene. “Unique on its own. Native to the whole.”
- **Scale:** End on a wider composition or rapid sequence of object families. “One language, from a single room to an endless world.”

### Initial prompt

Build **“Commonworld,”** an interactive 3D experience about creating an entire world from one shared visual language. Define a handcrafted Little Worlds grammar of soft geometric forms, captured panels, visible pegs and collars, functional seams, pale materials, and bright restrained accents. Apply that grammar consistently to objects, lighting, furniture, plants, storage, and architecture.

Let people create a new object from a photo or a few words. Interpret the evidence, design a unique silhouette, assemble it from the world’s allowed construction vocabulary, validate the result, and place it directly into the scene. Avoid generic fallback models and literal clones. Every result should contribute new variety while strengthening the identity of the whole world.

Begin with one open studio, but design the language to scale beyond it. The same rules should be capable of shaping a room, a street, a landscape, or an entire settlement. Make this scalability visible through families of objects and a scene whose architecture clearly belongs to the same system.

## Recommendation

**Use Little Worlds** if the aim is a warm, iconic, world-facing identity. It is the most ownable name, the most naturally demonstrable, and the one most likely to become inseparable from the visual language itself.

**Use Commonworld** if the ambition should read as larger and more foundational from the first encounter.

**Use Kinform** if the project should feel like a premium design system or future product rather than a playful world.

The strongest Showcase-ready pairing is:

> **Little Worlds**  
> *A playful world where every new object speaks the same handcrafted language.*
